The Man Who Threw Newspapers

Karthick was thirteen years old when he first encountered the man on the train.
It had been a day of triumph. For the first time, his mother had capitulated and granted him permission to cycle to school after two whole years of asking. 
The sticking point had always been the geography. The shortest route was five kilometres long and required navigation of a busy highway. A route his parents deemed far too treacherous for a child.
It was a very dry and uninspiring day, which was normal for a schoolkid on a Saturday. En route to the school, Karthick would need to cross a manned level crossing in the Malayampallam area. While the gate was open for most of the day, it possessed a frustrating habit of shutting down for five agonising minutes every morning around 7:45 a.m. The delay wasn’t caused by a train actually passing through but a train sitting and waiting. 
You see, just ahead of the train’s path was a now-derelict railway station, and right before it was an intersection. The 7:45 a.m. train was perpetually condemned to sit idle by the crossing for five minutes, waiting for its counterpart to clear the track before it could proceed.

Later on in his life, Karthick would attribute this daily absurdity to administrative stubbornness. But back then, he hadn’t yet learned to care about it.

That day, on Karthick’s inaugural cycle ride to school, he found himself waiting for the boom barrier, all alone.
It was an odd sensation. Somehow, in the middle of a bustling village that could rarely keep itself from being unnecessarily busy, he quite peculiarly found himself waiting alone by the crossing.

Well, that wasn’t true. He wasn’t quite alone. There was another person with him. In spirit, at least. 

Also waiting for the train to move was a man perched precariously in the open doorway of the idling train. Sitting right on the metal floor with his legs dangling playfully over the edge, he was occupied with folding his newspaper in a highly peculiar fashion. He wasn't doubling it over the way it had arrived. The way people usually folded newspapers. No. Instead, he would roll it tightly along the long edge and then snap it in half vertically. Karthick thought it weird, but he took no second notice of the habit. He was no authority on folded newspapers after all.

Karthick would also never know why he had fascinated the man that day. But somehow he had. 
Spotting a visibly bored teenager slumped over his handlebars, thoroughly exhausted by the stagnant wait, the man would suddenly break the silence.
"Hey! You!" the man would shout, gesturing toward him. "God’s Grace High School?”

“Yes,” Karthick would answer.

“Here. Read," and the man would get up from the dirty train floor and throw the awkwardly folded newspaper with incredible aim, landing right on Karthick himself. 
"Read the paper! It’s a good habit," the man would bark before retreating back into the train carriage.
What followed was somehow the most peculiar detail of all: the interior was entirely empty. In a country where literally all of the trains were notoriously packed to the gills, there was an abundance of open space to sit in this one. Making the man's choice to dangle his legs out of a moving doorway all the more bizarre.
Karthick was never one for newspapers. His own parents had attempted to instil the habit a few times with extremely limited success. His routine was unvarying. He would give a cursory glance at the sports section to glean headlines about games he had already watched live the night before. And that was it. He might spare a fleeting, ten-second look at the other sports headlines, but nothing ever truly held his gaze.

Naturally, that was exactly what he did the first time the man had thrown him the newspaper. He flipped straight to the match report from the previous night, where India had comfortably dismantled the West Indies. Rohit Sharma and Virat Kohli had stitched together a stellar partnership, completely bamboozling the Windies' bowling attack. And to be fair to him, he would not have the time to look further either. The boom barrier opened and let the public through, at which point he hurriedly stashed the newspaper in his bag and continued on his way to school.

His parents never questioned him about the newspaper that day. In fact, they hadn't even noticed the extra paper in the living room. Their anxieties were about Karthick’s safety with taking the cycle to school, which he insisted was fine, perfectly dandy and uneventful, which was completely true. It had been a safe and comfortable journey.

He would take the cycle again on the Monday but would not encounter that man and honestly? Karthick had forgotten about him already.
But on Tuesday, the man would reappear. This time, he was standing amidst a dense crowd of passengers, his face buried in the newspaper. Upon catching Karthick, who was not alone anymore but wedged tightly in between a mob of bikes, he would flash two fingers, signalling him to wait for a couple of minutes. Karthick watched him, thoroughly puzzled, until the man suddenly rolled and snapped his newspaper shut in that same bizarre ritual. Then, with that same uncanny, pinpoint accuracy, he launched it across the tracks straight at him.

“Read!”
Surprised by the man’s determination to make a reader out of him, he would open the sports page again, eager to see how the press had slaughtered his favourite player, who had delivered an absolute stinker of a performance the previous evening. But before he could finish the headline, the boom barrier lifted. Almost instantaneously, the mob of motorbikes erupted. Commuters swore harshly at the kid, aggressively telling him to get a move on, which he would do so begrudgingly. He had wanted to stay immobile just for a couple of minutes, just to irritate the mob.
It took another two months and five more airborne newspapers before Karthick finally summoned the curiosity to explore the front page. On his first attempt, however, he merely skimmed the headlines to find himself completely out of his depth and so promptly retreated to the familiar comfort of the sports section.
The very next day, however, he was determined to prove to himself that he wasn’t stupid. The moment the paper landed in his hands, he steered his bicycle over to a nearby tea stall, parked it, and claimed a spot on one of the benches. His sole mission: conquer the front page. He spent more than ten minutes painstakingly parsing through the dense text, a delay that put him dangerously close to being late for school. Yet, as he pedalled away, he felt an immense wave of satisfaction. He had done it.
Upon conquering the front page, he would then attempt to read the rest of the sports section before dabbling in the more rogue sides of the newspaper.

By four months, he was comfortable with reading half of it, and six months in, at the end of the school year, he would read almost all of the newspaper except the property and political sections. The man on the train had also developed the habit of using a rubber band to wrap the ends of the newspaper, making it easier for him to collect the paper in its full glory.

Throughout the summer holidays, he made a daily pilgrimage to the level crossing, waiting for his strange benefactor. And throw the newspaper, the man would, at least on the days their lucky orbits aligned.
Naturally, there were plenty of misses. There were days when the man simply wasn't on the train or perhaps was trapped in a different compartment. Other times, Karthick himself was tied up, arrived late, or missed the train’s arrival entirely. Then, there were those rare, miraculous instances when the train defied its own scheduling curse and didn't have to stop at the crossing at all.
The owner of the tea stall, a kind but impatient man named Gopi Sundar, would give Karthick as much time as he wanted to read the newspaper on his bench as long as he had a cup of tea in his hands. It was a trade-off Karthick thoroughly relished. Catching up on the news in the crisp morning air felt uniquely special. A wonderfully stark contrast to his old habit of scanning the pages during frantic school breaks or late in the evening, when the news had already gone stale, and reading could feel like a chore.

Within a year, Karthick had grown comfortable with the proposition of grazing headlines, developing a sharp instinct for tracking the stories across the newspaper. He would spend his five to ten minutes in the tea stall grasping every piece of news and reading only the interesting and important bits later on in the day. 

He rarely touched the newspapers his parents actually paid for. 
He didn’t feel an ounce of guilt about it either. Instead, he simply took extra care, making sure to dispose of his illicit reading material before heading home from school. Occasionally, he would pass his copy along to random commuters who looked like they wanted something to read. More often, though, he would smuggle the papers upstairs and hoard them on a secluded shelf. There they would sit, accumulating week by week, until he had a heavy enough bundle to quietly march down to the local recycler and trade them for a little independent pocket money, which he would more often than not use to treat himself to some chocolate and regret it later on about wasting the cash.

Karthick often wondered what the man’s name was, even though he never actively tried to find out. His urge to know the name mostly came from his growing sense of connection with the man. Along with the daily print, the man was inadvertently tossing over fragments of his own personality.

The sections the man had consumed with devotion and interest always arrived extra crumpled. Recognising this, Karthick began dedicating extra time to those exact pages, painstakingly smoothing out the creases as he did so.
Karthick had been actively trying to avoid the political section. He harboured a deep-seated dread of turning into someone like his maternal aunt, who was an insufferable, outspoken political zealot.
Whenever the paper arrived physically mangled, with entire sections violently torn away in what felt like sheer derision, Karthick found himself consumed by an irresistible curiosity. He desperately wanted to know what had provoked such a visceral reaction from the man on the train.
There was just one problem: by this point, the man had switched to a completely different daily newspaper, one that didn't match the subscription at home. Driven by a newfound obsession, Karthick bought a copy of that rival daily, purely to cross-reference what had been destroyed.
When he finally lined up the pages, the culprit invariably turned out to be a politically charged op-ed by a journalist fiercely criticising opposition leaders over highly sensitive issues. For the first time in his life, breaking his own golden rule, Karthick actually sat down and read a political piece.
From that afternoon onwards, he stopped turning a blind eye to those pages. As the years bled into one another, his understanding of the man’s politics deepened. A worldview pieced together entirely from the wrinkles and tears of a discarded daily chronicle. He would realise that he and the man on the train would not see eye-to-eye on many contemporary talking points, but it did not stop him from being curious.

Some days, the man would tear out certain advertisements. Clearly items he intended to follow up on himself. These excised blocks were almost always promotions for shoes, listings for gold, or, on one peculiar morning, a standard obituary.
Karthick was certain that the man didn’t know that he was actively spending effort trying to find out the things the man had blocked him from knowing. Which was probably why he had stripped out the obituary. He was confident that if he had followed up on the dead person, he would know who the man on the train was. But he didn't.

The first time that Karthick put in his own money to buy and read the newspapers was when he turned eighteen. It was the year he had to pack his bags, leave the familiar rhythms of the Kollengode area behind, and head north to Pilani, where he had won admission at the prestigious Birla Institute of Technology. It was also at this point that Karthick had realised he was no longer reading the newspapers, not because the old man had thrown them for him, but because he wanted to know what had happened in the world before the world had the chance to tell him.

It was four months before he went to the level crossing again, full of excitement at the prospect of seeing the man again. But, as it turned out, he wouldn’t. The man did not appear that day.

In fact, he met the man only one more time. And that was in his third year of college. And he wasn’t even going there during the morning. Riding the exhilarating high of a boy whose crush, Shruti, had finally accepted his love, Karthick found himself drawn back to the level crossing in the evening, guided entirely by instinct. It was there, quite unexpectedly, that he spotted him. The man was on a completely different train, once again perched on the metal floor, methodically folding his newspaper.
He looked visibly older now, the years having clearly taken their toll. Yet, the moment his eyes landed on Karthick, his face lit up with pure, unadulterated joy. With considerably less precision than in the old days and much more time taken to align his throw, he launched the newspaper across the gap one final time, his voice cutting through the dusk with a singular, triumphant command:
"READ!"
And read that he did. Almost every word of it. He silently regretted not waving goodbye in that moment, because he was fairly sure, and rightfully so, that it was his last time seeing the old man.

By the time he completed his bachelor’s degree and travelled north once more to pursue his master's, word reached him that the old level crossing was being dismantled. 
Progress had finally caught up to the junction; a massive road-over-bridge was under construction, designed so that commuters would never have to idly wait for a train again. It was a milestone of urban development that he knew almost everyone back home would be celebrating. He did not join them in doing so.
It wasn’t the newspapers that had disappeared first. It was the waiting.

As he turned twenty-eight, he made one final visit to his childhood home in Kollengode for his wedding to his college sweetheart. Walking up to the newly inaugurated bridge, a project that had taken the best part of half a decade to complete, he felt like an outsider. And that was despite being someone who had traversed this exact route for almost his entire childhood. Down below the bridge, the tea seller still sold the morning paper, and Karthick bought one, settling in to read. 

It didn’t feel the same anymore, though. The pages lay perfectly flat. There were no stubborn, curly edges fighting to roll back up from a stranger’s tight grip. There were no random, heavily crumpled sections to decipher, no articles torn away in derision. But it would have to do.
And so, as he turned thirty-two, with his two-year-old son, Mithun, balance-walking against his knee, Karthick felt a sudden, heavy wave of remorse as he picked up the morning paper. He knew, with absolute certainty, that it was the very last one.
Over the course of the past year, nearly every major news agency had thrown in the towel, declaring it anachronistic and stupid to keep printing physical editions in the high-tech landscape of 2033. One by one, they had migrated entirely to the digital world. Only a stubborn couple of publications had held out, a number that eventually dwindled to a lone survivor: The Bharat Chronicles.
But today, even they had surrendered. Sprawled across the top of their own front page was the definitive announcement: this was the final print edition they would ever run. Their press was also closing.
He stared at the page for a long time, his eyes tracing every sentence, reading every single word until the ink practically blurred. He would unconsciously try to smudge the ink off the paper and fail at it, only managing to produce a small tear on the paper.
Reading the news had always been a fundamentally different experience back when the pages arrived battle-scarred. Full of deep wrinkles, tight curls, and sections violently torn away by the man's hand. Yet, even in its pristine, sterile modern form, it was a tangible anchor to his youth. Now, facing the stark reality that he could never do it again, he stared down at the crisp paper, wondering what on earth to do with the final piece of his dying world.

Surprisingly, the answer was simple. He would go to the balcony where he would find a group of boys, sitting on a bench, clearly waiting for their friends to show up before they could head for school.
“Hey, you guys? Which school do you go to?” he shouted across the yard.
The kids stopped, looking up in mild confusion before calling back, "Beacon Academy".
Satisfied, he would roll the newspaper along its long, vertical axis, crease it firmly down the middle, and snap it together with a rogue rubber band he’d scavenged from the table. With an aim that was not nearly as good as the man on the train’s was, he tossed it out to the children. They caught it awkwardly, staring down at the tight cylinder as if they had never seen physical newsprint before in their lives.
“Read the paper. It’s a good habit,” he called out to them, a faint smile breaking across his face before he turned and stepped back into the house.
Inside, he caught Shruti watching him from the doorway, her expression brimming with curiosity. He didn't say a word. He wasn't going to tell her the story of the man on the train. Not today. That was a sacred story meant for him alone. A private myth that no one else would understand and bury him under a million questions with.
So, Karthick kept his silence. And the truth was, he would never speak a single word about that mysterious benefactor until the day he reached the exact same age as the man himself. Only then, greying and wise, would he finally sit down and begin telling the story of the man who threw newspapers.
